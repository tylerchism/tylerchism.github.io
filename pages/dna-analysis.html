Folate / B12 Pathway
The Methylation Cascade
Impaired at Every Gate — tap any item to understand what's breaking and why it matters
  • 1
    Folate gets in slowly — SLC19A1/RFC1 transporter (likely hom)
    What's happening & what it means
    What's breaking
    The protein that ferries folate from food across your gut wall into your bloodstream works at reduced capacity. Folate still gets in — just at a lower rate per serving.
    Dietary lever
    Prioritize high-folate foods: liver, dark leafy greens (raw — cooking destroys folate), avocado. Eating more of these foods still helps because you're making up for throughput, not a total block.
    Health implication
    Lower folate input means less raw material entering the whole downstream cascade. Every gate after this is already working from a deficit starting point.
  • 2
    Folate gets trapped — MTHFS (likely hom)
    What's happening & what it means
    What's breaking
    Folate in your body exists in several forms. One form (5-formyl-THF) is like folate that's been put in a locked box — it can't be used for methylation until an enzyme unlocks it. Your MTHFS variant means that enzyme works poorly, so folate piles up in the locked box, unusable.
    Dietary lever
    Eating more folate doesn't fix this — you'd just accumulate more trapped folate. The only workaround is folinic acid (a specific supplement form) which enters the pathway downstream of this lock.
    Health implication
    Paradoxically, high folate intake without the right supplement can make this worse — the trapped form competes with usable forms at receptor sites. This is part of why synthetic folic acid (in fortified foods) is a problem for your genetics specifically.
  • 3
    Mitochondria can't run their folate loop — MTHFD1 (likely hom)
    What's happening & what it means
    What's breaking
    Your mitochondria run their own separate folate cycle to build DNA building blocks (purines) and to transfer carbon units for energy production. MTHFD1 runs that cycle. When it's impaired, mitochondria can't make enough of their own raw materials.
    Dietary lever
    Serine-rich foods (meat, eggs, dairy) feed alternative carbon-transfer paths. This is one reason a protein-forward diet makes intuitive sense for your genetics — you're compensating for reduced mitochondrial self-sufficiency.
    Health implication
    This one hits energy directly, not just methylation. Impaired MTHFD1 means your mitochondria are running a constrained operation even before we get to the Complex I issues covered in slide 7. Fatigue that doesn't resolve with rest is one downstream signal.
  • 4
    Core methylation enzyme at ~30% capacity — MTHFR C677T CONFIRMED HOM
    What's happening & what it means
    What's breaking
    MTHFR converts folate into its active methylating form (5-MTHF). This is the central conversion step the whole system depends on. Being homozygous for C677T means the enzyme works at roughly 30% of normal activity. This is confirmed at maximum confidence by both SIFT and PolyPhen-2.
    Dietary lever
    Riboflavin (B2) is MTHFR's required cofactor — without enough B2, even the 30% that remains can't run efficiently. Liver, dairy, eggs, and almonds are B2-rich. This is also why B2 supplementation (25–50 mg) has outsized benefit for C677T carriers specifically. Natural folate from food is still valuable; synthetic folic acid is counterproductive (see slide 6).
    Health implication
    Everything methylation-dependent gets throttled here: SAM production, homocysteine clearance, DNA methylation patterns, neurotransmitter synthesis. Elevated homocysteine is the most testable downstream marker — testing this number is the most important lab you can order.
  • 5
    Less B12 extracted from food — GIF (likely het)
    What's happening & what it means
    What's breaking
    Intrinsic factor (GIF) is a protein your stomach secretes that grabs B12 from food and escorts it to the absorption site in your small intestine. Reduced intrinsic factor = less B12 extracted per meal, even if you eat B12-rich foods.
    Dietary lever
    Beef liver, oysters, clams, and sardines are extremely B12-dense — eating these regularly still helps because you're compensating for absorption efficiency with volume. Rare/less-cooked preparations preserve B12 better than well-done. High-dose sublingual B12 bypasses this entirely (absorbed directly through mouth tissue, no stomach acid or intrinsic factor needed).
    Health implication
    A B12 blood test can look "normal" while functional deficiency exists at the cell level — because most B12 tests measure total B12, not active B12 in use. Elevated MMA (methylmalonic acid) is a better functional marker.
  • 6
    B12 delivery to cells impaired — TCN2 (het confirmed)
    What's happening & what it means
    What's breaking
    Transcobalamin II is a transport protein that carries B12 through the bloodstream to your cells. Even the B12 you do absorb doesn't deliver as efficiently. Think of it as a reduced fleet of B12 taxis — some passengers (B12) are left waiting.
    Dietary lever
    No strong dietary workaround for transport — you compensate by keeping B12 levels higher so even reduced transport capacity is carrying adequate load. This is another reason sublingual methylcobalamin at therapeutic doses (not RDA levels) makes sense.
    Health implication
    This stacks on top of Gate 5. Two independent impairments on the B12 supply chain before you even get to Gate 7, which handles recycling.
  • 7
    B12 gets used up and can't be recycled — MTRR Ile22Met CONFIRMED HOM
    What's happening & what it means
    What's breaking
    MTRR regenerates active B12 from its spent form. Every time B12 donates a methyl group, it ends up oxidized and unusable until MTRR recharges it. Your homozygous MTRR variant means this recharging barely works — so B12 drains rapidly even if your levels look adequate. SIFT score of 0.000 means all three major predictive algorithms rate this at maximum deleteriousness.
    Dietary lever
    No food fixes poor recycling directly. The only approach is to constantly replenish active B12 faster than it drains — hence the need for sublingual methylcobalamin supplementation, not just dietary B12. Keeping total B12 high in the system gives MTRR enough substrate to work with even at reduced efficiency.
    Health implication
    This is the hardest gate to compensate for through diet alone, and it compounds with Gates 5 and 6. Together, these three B12 gates mean functional B12 deficiency is the default state without active intervention, regardless of how much B12-rich food you eat.

Biochemical Cascade
What Happens When the Factory Runs Low
SAM is the product of that broken pipeline — and it runs everything below
How this follows from slide 4: Every gate we just saw feeds into producing SAM (S-adenosylmethionine) — the body's universal methyl donor. SAM is like a molecular postal service that delivers "methyl groups" to hundreds of targets. When the factory (slide 4) is broken at every stage, the postal service runs chronically understaffed. This slide shows what stops getting delivered.

SAM Needs to Reach...

  • 🧬DNA methylation — controls which genes get turned on or off. Low SAM = dysregulated gene expression.
  • 🧠Neurotransmitters — dopamine and serotonin synthesis both require methylation steps. Low SAM = neurotransmitter bottleneck.
  • 🔬Brain membranes — phospholipid synthesis for neuronal membrane health
  • 🧹Detoxification — methylation deactivates estrogens, histamine, environmental toxins
  • ⚡Mitochondrial membranes — membrane biogenesis requires methylation
  • 💊CoQ10 synthesis — a methylation step is required to make CoQ10, your primary mitochondrial antioxidant/electron carrier

What to Watch For

  • 🔴Elevated homocysteine — the most direct measurable signal of low SAM. Target < 7 µmol/L. Get this tested.
  • 🔴Brain fog and mood instability — especially under stress when methylation demand spikes
  • 🔴Poor toxin and estrogen clearance — sluggish detox, hormonal sensitivity
  • 🔴Impaired DNA repair — the signaling that coordinates cellular repair is methylation-dependent

The COMT Compounding Effect

COMT (your stress-dopamine clearance enzyme) requires SAM to work. Low SAM means COMT runs slower regardless of your genotype — so stress hits harder and takes longer to metabolically clear. You may feel stress disproportionately relative to the situation.

"The downstream consequence is chronically suboptimal SAM, elevated homocysteine, and impaired methylation of DNA, proteins, neurotransmitters, and mitochondrial membranes."

🚫 Supplement Forms & Stacks to Avoid
  • Synthetic folic acid in any supplement — can't be processed by your blocked MTHFR. Piles up as unmetabolized folic acid (UMFA), competing with the active forms you actually need. Hidden in many "prenatal," generic B-complex, and multivitamin products — read every label.
  • Methylfolate without adequate B12 support — pushing in methylfolate without the downstream methylation cycle consuming it backs up 5,10-methylene-THF further. Always pair with active B12.
  • High-dose methylfolate on a carnivore/high-red-meat diet — Chris Masterjohn's specific flag for this genetic profile: red meat already loads the sulfur amino acid pathways. Adding high-dose methylfolate on top is "pouring fuel on the fire" — drives excess sulfur catabolism, which can cause mitochondrial toxicity and neurological agitation (insomnia, restlessness). Start very low (200–400 mcg) and build. Folinic acid is the gentler entry point.
  • Methylcobalamin as your only B12 form (at first) — methylcobalamin adds methyl groups directly. A sudden bolus can cause over-methylation symptoms (agitation, anxiety, insomnia) before the system rebalances. Hydroxocobalamin is metabolically neutral — your body converts it to whatever form it currently needs. Safer starting point when first calibrating this protocol.
  • Standard B-complex stacks — most off-the-shelf B-complex products contain synthetic folic acid + cyanocobalamin (least bioavailable B12 form) + broad B vitamins at doses that stress sulfur pathways. Build a targeted stack rather than a generic "B-100" product.
💡 Masterjohn note on creatine: Creatine synthesis consumes a large share of your daily methyl groups. Supplementing creatine (start at 1g/day, not the standard 5g loading dose — many people with methylation issues are sensitive to large doses) reduces that demand, sparing SAMe for everything downstream.
☀️ Secondary Protocol — Vitamin D
Your genome shows a two-hit situation in the vitamin D pathway — separate from methylation but worth addressing:
  • Your vitamin D transporter (VDBP/GC) binds D less tightly — the Gc2 haplotype variant means vitamin D circulates less efficiently through your bloodstream. You may show adequate 25-OH-D on a lab result while tissue delivery is still suboptimal.
  • Your activation enzyme (CYP27B1) runs below capacity — this enzyme converts storage-form D into active hormonal D. Reduced activity means the conversion step itself is a bottleneck, independent of how much D you get from sun or diet.
  • Your D receptor (VDR) is fine — when active D does arrive at the receptor, it binds and works normally. The problem is upstream of the receptor.
What this means practically: You likely need more D than average to achieve the same tissue effect. Don't rely on "adequate" sun exposure alone. Target serum 25-OH-D of 50–80 ng/mL (higher end of optimal), not just the 30 ng/mL lab reference floor.
D3 + K2 (MK-7) — take together, not separately. D3 at higher doses (2,000–5,000 IU/day depending on baseline) mobilizes calcium. K2 (MK-7, 100–200 mcg/day) directs that calcium into bone and away from soft tissue. High-dose D3 without K2 is the combination to avoid.
📋 Lab to run: 25-OH-D3 serum baseline before adjusting dosage — test quarterly until stable in target range.
Eggs, liver, and beets cover a lot of ground. The supplements exist to handle what your specific genetic blocks make impossible to fully fix through food.

The Three-Way Compound Burden

Your Dietary Options for Energy
Full ketosis is the mechanistic optimum — but there are other effective approaches if that's not where you are

Option A: Full Ketosis (Best Mechanistically)

Dietary fat >70%, carbs <30g/day. Produces ketones that bypass Complex I entirely. Your genetic advantage on fat is most pronounced here. Best for sustained cognitive output and steady energy without crashes.

Option B: Strategic Carb Timing (If Not Going Full Keto)

  • ✅Post-workout carbs only — after resistance training, muscle uptake is insulin-independent and rapid. Your Complex I handles the brief burst; it's the sustained load that creates problems.
  • ✅Fat-dominant meals outside workout windows — breakfast and lunch as fat + protein; carbs at dinner or post-workout if used at all
  • ✅Fasting before cognitive work — even an overnight fast puts you in a mild ketogenic state; brain runs cleaner
  • ✅Protein adequacy — amino acids (glutamine, leucine) support alternative energy paths and spare glucose
🚫 Combinations That Hit Hardest for Your Genetics
  • High carb + high stress — cortisol drives glucose oxidation exactly when your Complex I is already stressed by cortisol's own energy demands. Double burden.
  • High carb + poor sleep — sleep deprivation impairs mitochondrial efficiency baseline. Adding glucose dependency compounds this significantly.
  • High carb + sustained endurance cardio — steady-state aerobic exercise is the most Complex I-dependent activity. This is where fat-adapted athletes have the clearest genetic advantage over you in a glucose-fueled state.
  • High sugar + stimulants (caffeine) — stimulant-driven performance on a glucose substrate is borrowed energy. Fat-adapted caffeine use is more stable and sustainable for your biology.
Resistance training builds more metabolically flexible muscle tissue. More muscle = better fat oxidation capacity even between sessions. This is a leverage point regardless of which dietary approach you take.

🔴 DOUBLE-HOM

Cellular Cleanup Gene — Both copies, both variants impaired

Your body runs a process called autophagy — essentially cellular housekeeping where cells identify, dismantle, and recycle damaged components before they cause problems. Think of it as a garbage collection system. The ATG16L1 protein is a structural piece of the machinery that does this work. You have two different genetic variants in this gene, and for both, you inherited the impaired version from both parents — so both copies are affected. The machinery runs below spec.

Variants: rs2241880 (T300A) and rs10210302, both homozygous (+/+)
