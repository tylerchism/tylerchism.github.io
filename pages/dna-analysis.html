🚫 What to Avoid (or Approach Carefully)
  • Synthetic folic acid — can't be processed by your blocked MTHFR. Piles up as unmetabolized folic acid (UMFA), competing with the active forms you actually need. Hidden in fortified foods, enriched breads, many "prenatal" and generic B-complex supplements — read every label.
  • Methylfolate without adequate B12 support — on its own, methylfolate can worsen the folate trap. Your MTHFR block means you can't recycle 5-methyl-THF back to THF unless the downstream methylation cycle (driven by B12) can consume it. Pushing in more methylfolate without that drain available backs up 5,10-methylene-THF further. Always pair with active B12.
  • High-dose methylfolate on a carnivore/high-red-meat diet — Chris Masterjohn's specific flag for this genetic profile: red meat already loads the sulfur amino acid pathways. Adding high-dose methylfolate on top of that is "pouring fuel on the fire" — the combination drives excess sulfur catabolism, which can cause mitochondrial toxicity and neurological agitation (insomnia, restlessness). Start very low (200–400 mcg) and build. Folinic acid is the gentler entry point.
  • Methylcobalamin as your only B12 form — methylcobalamin adds methyl groups directly into the system. If you're already adapted to low methyl supply, a sudden bolus of methylcobalamin can cause over-methylation symptoms (agitation, anxiety, insomnia) before the system rebalances. Hydroxocobalamin is metabolically neutral — your body converts it to whatever form it currently needs. It's the safer starting point, especially when first calibrating this protocol.
  • Standard B-complex stacks — most off-the-shelf B-complex products contain synthetic folic acid + cyanocobalamin (the least bioavailable B12 form) + high doses of multiple B vitamins that can stress sulfur pathways. If you want dietary B vitamins, favor food sources: liver is the most nutrient-dense single source. If supplementing, build a targeted stack rather than a generic "B-100" product.
  • Fortified nutritional yeast — unfortified nutritional yeast is a reasonable food-based B vitamin source. Most commercial brands are fortified with synthetic B vitamins. Seek explicitly unfortified varieties.
💡 Masterjohn note on creatine: Creatine synthesis consumes a large share of your daily methyl groups. Supplementing creatine reduces that demand, sparing SAMe for everything downstream. On a high-protein diet you're getting some creatine from meat, but supplemental creatine (start at 1g/day, not the standard 5g "loading" dose — many people with methylation issues are sensitive to large doses) can meaningfully relieve MTHFR burden independent of any folate supplement.
☀️ Secondary Protocol — Vitamin D
Your genome shows a two-hit situation in the vitamin D pathway — separate from methylation but worth addressing:
  • Your vitamin D transporter (VDBP/GC) binds D less tightly — the Gc2 haplotype variant means vitamin D circulates less efficiently through your bloodstream. You may show adequate 25-OH-D on a lab result while tissue delivery is still suboptimal.
  • Your activation enzyme (CYP27B1) runs below capacity — this is the enzyme that converts the storage form of D into the active, hormonal form. Reduced activity means the conversion step itself is a bottleneck, independent of how much D you get from sun or diet.
  • Your D receptor (VDR) is fine — when active D does arrive at the receptor, it binds and works normally. The problem is upstream of the receptor.
What this means practically: You likely need more D than average to achieve the same tissue effect. Don't rely on "adequate" sun exposure alone. Target serum 25-OH-D of 50–80 ng/mL (higher end of optimal), not just the 30 ng/mL lab reference floor.
D3 + K2 (MK-7) — take together, not separately. D3 at higher doses (2,000–5,000 IU/day depending on baseline serum level) mobilizes calcium. K2 (MK-7 form, 100–200 mcg/day) directs that calcium into bone and away from soft tissue. High-dose D3 without K2 is the combination to avoid — it raises calcium without directing it. This pairing also complements the methylation protocol since K2 is involved in vitamin K-dependent carboxylation pathways.
📋 Lab to run: 25-OH-D3 serum baseline before adjusting dosage — test quarterly until stable in target range.
